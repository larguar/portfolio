10 Takeaways from An Event Apart 2017

On Monday and Tuesday I attended An Event Apart DC for the first time (which was actually in Alexandria, VA) – a 2 day conference with 6 speakers per day, or as the branded dixie cups described, “The design conference for people who make websites”.

It was me, my iPad (and Apple Pencil, duh), the ACS Fairfax team, and a huge room full of designers and developers from all over the place with a variety of experience levels and punny t-shirts. I felt small, but excited.

Day 1 of the event featured Jeffrey Zeldman, co-founder of the event, Stephanie Hay, Daniel Burka, Dan Mall, Rachel Andrew, and Jen Simmons. Day 2 featured Val Head, Jason Grigsby, Una Kravets, Derek Featherstone, Eric Meyer, and Gerry McGovern. The speakers were super great and by the end of it, I no longer felt small. I had a lot to go home and think about.

Here are the 10 takeaways I wanted to share:

01. Research is super important.

Just because you like a website that matches the category of the site you’re creating doesn’t mean your site should look like that. You may be imitating an anti-user website. Research is the easiest path to creating the best website for the needs of the customer.

"Research is a great way because it's a humble way." @zeldman #AEADC

— Liz Sabatiuk (@mobilia5) July 10, 2017

02. Content defines structure, not the other way around.

We should be storytelling before the design even starts – design is bringing the story to life. Designing for understanding boosts business AND emotion and that should be your #1 goal.

Starting with the content, we understand the story before creating the experience that leads us there. #aeadc @steph_hay

— Claire Blaustein (@museful) July 10, 2017

03. Yes, designers need to know how to code.

If you’re a designer that doesn’t know how to code, it’s not the end of the world (yet). At the very least, you should teach yourself CSS and JSON and you’ll make your developer’s life so much easier.

For designers just learning to code: play in the web inspector on someone else’s website. @danmall #aeadc

— zeldman (@zeldman) July 10, 2017

04. Asymmetry is cool.

When it comes to websites, you don’t HAVE to line things up with a grid. Design has the flexibility for our creative freedom and CSS Grid allows for this flexibility on the web. It is important, however, to be designing things through a device’s viewport rather than the full picture. Think storyboarding.

05. CSS is flexible too.

When it comes to flexibility, you don’t need to stick to % and px or ems as your units. Vmin, vh, vw, max-content, min-content, minmax, and fr take away most of the thinking and math involved with building the perfect grid. If you haven’t used these before, just start experimenting.

@jensimmons is practically begging us to use something other than 12 equal columns… oh and WHITE SPACE! #aeadc #aneventapart

— Benjamin Aaron Myers (@benjaminamyers) July 10, 2017

New CSS moving from px/em/% to min or max content - responding to the content instead of the frame. More reason for #contentfirst? #aeadc

— Claire Blaustein (@museful) July 10, 2017

06. Animation really does make your website a lot cooler.

You can build an animation into your website with CSS, JS, SVG, or a combination of the three, and it’s not as difficult as it may seem. Keyframes are reusable, JS animation libraries already exist for you to use, and there are plenty of tools you can find online to help you.

JavaScript is great at * Complex animated interactions * Narrative or immersive animation * Dynamic state transitions @vlh #aeadc

— zeldman (@zeldman) July 11, 2017

SVG’s great at

* Animated illustrations
* Animated icons
* Infographics
* Fluid scaling@vlh #webanimation #aeadc

— zeldman (@zeldman) July 11, 2017

07. Image optimization is a team problem.

If your site needs a page loader, your site is too slow and needs to be better optimized. It’s not up to the client to provide you with images that are optimized for web – that’s your job.

08. Eventually it’ll become second nature.

There are so many simple ways (and tools) to optimize your images for web. B&W images are smaller files, so use CSS Blend Modes to your advantage. If you subtly blur the unimportant parts of an image it’ll save a ton of image space (wow !!).

Mobile sites that loaded within 5 secs vs 19 secs were observed to have 70% longer average sessions #aeadc @Una

— craig zdanowicz (@djbuffnstuff) July 11, 2017

Access to information is a human right. By ignoring performance, our sites prevent many in the world from exercising that right. @una #aeadc

— zeldman (@zeldman) July 11, 2017

09. Compliance is not the goal.

Our goal is for people to be able to use the stuff we make (otherwise, what’s the point?). There are so many disabilities in the world and it’s important that no one is excluded. Keyboard issues, image/media issues, and form issues are the 3 most common.

Accessibility lives in people.
Accessibility lives in process.
Accessibility lives in tools.#aeadc @feather

— Claire Blaustein (@museful) July 11, 2017

10. It’s all about the customer.

With usability measurement sessions, measure from the point of view of the customer, not yourself and not the client. Testing with 3-8 people can identify the problem, testing with 13-18 can put a % number on the problem.

You don’t need to see your test subjects’ facial expressions. If their cursor is all over the page, you *know* they’re confused. #aeadc

— zeldman (@zeldman) July 11, 2017

By the end of the 2nd speaker, my creative juices were flowing flooding and I just wanted to start making and tweaking. The whole time I just kept thinking, “good thing I didn’t bring my laptop”. Instead I took advantage of all of the free snacks and coffee, soaked up as much information as I possibly could in 2 days, and had an incredible experience at An Event Apart 2017.

I’m excited for what’s to come.
