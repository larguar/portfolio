An Event Apart: Jen Simmons

As I explained in my last post, I attended a design conference this week called An Event Apart in Alexandria, VA. An Event Apart is a 2 day conference with 6 speakers per day, otherwise known as “The design conference for people who make websites”.

I wanted to recap one specific presentation by Jen Simmons, Designer Advocate at Mozilla. Jen, for a living, advocates for web standards and researches the future of web (basically). She also does talks pretty frequently and hosts a podcast called The Web Ahead.

Here are my 2 major takeaways from her talk:

01. Our medium is not done.

The design of web pages have evolved drastically over the last couple of decades. We started at a place where no one really knew what websites were, what they could do, and what the point was. These websites were extremely basic HTML sites.

Then people started to figure out HTML and all of the cool stuff we could do with it (flashback to me in middle school with my super hip Myspace page). This was also the introduction of Flash, which gave people a new creative freedom. Think rainbow gradient text, table-based layouts, marquees and not-so-subtle drop shadows. It was ugly, but it was new and it was exciting.

In the early 2000s, people started using this newly supported shiny thing called CSS (ooooh) and this allowed for a separation of content and design. Eventually JavaScript and jQuery were introduced into the web world and – fast forward to now:

Design and the web are always evolving and websites today are a lot different then they were even just a couple of years ago. Oh, and mobile web is also a really big thing now so we have to build out our websites responsively. With websites today, there’s a whole lot more thinking and planning involved in order to organize content in the most efficient way possible for the end customer.

That being said, we don’t have to design websites the way that other people are designing them. We don’t have to line things up with grid if we don’t want to. We can be more creative than that. We don’t have all of the answers, so why not design a website that tells its story through a fun, asymmetrical format? Think magazine layouts. If you can mock it up, it can be built out on the web.

02. We should be designing exactly what we see on the screen.

On any given day, I’m looking at sites on an iPhone 7, iPad Pro, 13″ Macbook Air, and a 27″ iMac. When I’m designing website pages, I’m starting with a 1400pt x 1024pt artboard and stretching it vertically until I’m finished with my design.

We need to design through a viewport. How do things look as they scroll into view? #aeadc @jensimmons

— Dori Kelner (@dorikelner) July 10, 2017

What we need to be doing is designing things through a viewport. This is what videographers and animators do on a regular basis – they storyboard. We, as designers should be storyboarding and designing exactly what’s on the screen at any given time and any given device.

Viewport responsibility is so important, and it shouldn’t only be applied to the homepage either. You want to make an impression no matter which page the customer stumbles upon. You are curating a conscious experience.

Anyway, Jen Simmons is really brilliant when it comes to CSS Grid and layout and public speaking. I really enjoyed her talk and I’d recommend following her on Twitter and keeping up with her experiments.
